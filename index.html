Queensland (QLD)

Most QLD state high schools run a mandatory BYOD laptop program. The program is called BYOx (Bring Your Own x) by the Department of Education.

  • Chromebooks and Android tablets are not supported on the EQ network at most state schools — a Windows or Mac laptop is usually required.
  • Microsoft Office 365 is free for all Queensland state school students.
  • Some schools use a CYOD (Choose Your Own Device) or school-managed laptop program as a paid alternative to BYOD — useful if a family needs a school to source the device.
  • A small number of schools (e.g. Brisbane State High, Wynnum junior years, Ormeau Woods) require an iPad instead of a laptop — always check the year level.
  • A few schools have formal hardship or access programs (e.g. Rochedale SHS, Caboolture SHS) where daily loan devices or hire schemes are available.
  • QLD high schools cover Years 7–12 in one school — no separate senior college system.
Tasmania (TAS)

Tasmania calls the program BYOT (Bring Your Own Technology). Key differences from other states:

  • BYOT is optional at most Tasmanian government high schools — families are not required to provide a device. Schools have shared devices available.
  • Tasmania has a three-tier system: primary school (K–6), high school (Years 7–10), then separate colleges for Years 11–12. The colleges are in Hobart, Launceston, Devonport and Burnie.
  • If a family's child is in Year 11 or 12, they attend a college, not a high school — search for the college name (e.g. Hobart College, Launceston College, Don College, Hellyer College).
  • Some government high schools (e.g. Brighton High School) provide school-issued laptops to all students — no family purchase needed.
  • Parklands High School (Devonport) has a formal laptop hire scheme with instalment payment options.
Western Australia (WA)

WA state high schools cover Years 7–12 in one school (no separate senior college). BYOD is common but policies vary significantly by school.

  • Secondary Assistance Scheme (SAS) — a WA government hardship program available to low-income families (Centrelink/concession card holders) with children in Years 7–12 at any WA public or non-government school. Families must apply at the school each year. This is automatically noted on every WA school result in this tool.
  • WA Student Assistance Payment — a separate annual payment available to all WA school families, regardless of income. Apply via the ServiceWA app each year.
  • Microsoft Office 365 is free for all WA public school students.
  • Some WA schools use local suppliers like Stott Hoare or CDM as preferred BYOD vendors with service packages — these can be a practical option for families needing help sourcing a device.
  • Chromebooks and iPads are not supported at most WA state schools — Windows laptop is the standard requirement.
  • Variety WA offers technology grants for families in need — worth flagging to caseworkers when a family cannot afford a device.
South Australia (SA)

SA secondary schools cover Years 7–12 in one school — no separate senior college system. Microsoft Office 365 is free for all SA government school students.

  • School Card (critical for Zephyr): SA low-income families on School Card are eligible for a free device under the SA Department for Education's School Device Program. Year 7 and Year 10 School Card holders are prioritised. Families must apply for School Card at their school each year. This is automatically noted on every SA school result in this tool.
  • 1:1 device target by end 2026: The SA Department requires all secondary schools to have a 1:1 device ratio by end of 2026. This means most schools are actively working toward every student having a device — shared/loan devices are becoming more available.
  • LWT (Learning with Technologies) is the most common BYOD supplier across SA government schools. Many schools offer rent-to-buy monthly payment plans through LWT — a practical option for families who cannot afford upfront purchase.
  • Chromebooks are rarely supported at SA government schools — Windows or Mac is the standard requirement. Springbank Secondary College is a notable exception (they chose Chromebook to save families ~$800).
  • Some schools (e.g. Adelaide Botanic High School, Westminster School) are not BYOD — they provide school-issued devices to all students. Always check the result card.
  • Unley High School has a two-device policy — iPad mandatory in Years 7–9, then laptop from Year 10. Plan accordingly when sourcing a device.
  • Woodville High School has outstanding hardship support — free laptop for School Card holders in Year 7 and Year 10, plus daily loan devices for students without access.
Northern Territory (NT)

The NT has a small number of secondary schools concentrated in Darwin, Palmerston, Alice Springs, and Katherine. It is undergoing significant restructuring from Term 1, 2026.

  • Major school reform from 2026: The NT is merging middle schools (Yr 7–9) and senior colleges (Yr 10–12) into comprehensive Yr 7–12 high schools. Darwin Middle + Darwin High School → Darwin High School. Nightcliff Middle → Nightcliff High School. Sanderson Middle → Sanderson High School. Palmerston College → Driver Secondary School + Rosebery Secondary School. Casuarina Senior College retains its name as a Yr 10–12 school.
  • BYOD is mostly optional at NT government schools — schools maintain computer labs and shared devices for all students. BYOD is encouraged but rarely mandatory.
  • Microsoft Office 365 is free for all NT government school students.
  • NTCET (NT Certificate of Education and Training) is the senior qualification — based on SA's SACE curriculum. The exception is Haileybury Rendall School which uses VCE (Victorian curriculum).
  • Remote community schools: A large number of NT schools serve remote Aboriginal communities. BYOD programs are generally not applicable — these schools are resourced directly by the NT government. For families in remote communities, contact the NT Dept of Education on 1800 246 468.
  • Haileybury Rendall School is NT's top-performing school and the largest Indigenous boarding school in Australia. It serves remote community students alongside Darwin day students.
New South Wales (NSW)

NSW is Australia's largest state. All high schools cover Years 7–12 in one school. BYOD is near-universal across NSW government secondary schools.

  • Free software (important): All NSW Dept of Education students get Microsoft Office 365 AND Adobe Creative Cloud free. There is no need to purchase any software — this significantly reduces the cost of a BYOD setup.
  • 5GHz WiFi requirement: NSW Dept of Education's school WiFi runs on 5GHz 802.11n. Devices must support 5GHz wireless — a common mistake is buying a cheap device that only supports 2.4GHz and cannot connect to school networks.
  • Android tablets not recommended at most NSW government schools — proxy compatibility issues mean many school resources won't work. Chromebooks work at some schools but have limitations.
  • No state-wide free device program — unlike SA (School Card) or WA (SAS), NSW has no central government hardship device scheme. However, most schools maintain loan laptop pools for equity/hardship — contact the school directly. Some schools (e.g. Ulladulla High, Nowra High) have formal loan schemes with financial assistance forms.
  • Selective schools: NSW has about 50 government academically selective high schools. All run BYOD programs — specifications vary by school.
  • JB Hi-Fi Education is the most common purchasing portal across NSW government schools, with school-specific codes. LWT (Learning with Technologies) is also used.
  • NSW Certificate of Education (HSC) is the senior qualification, awarded at the end of Year 12.
Australian Capital Territory (ACT)

ACT has a unique structure — high schools run Years 7–10 only, with separate colleges for Years 11–12. Students choose a college after completing Year 10.

  • FREE Chromebook for all public secondary students: Every ACT public school student in Years 7–12 receives a free government-owned Chromebook under the Device Access & Equity (DAE) program. Families do NOT need to purchase a device. This is the single most important fact for Zephyr families in the ACT.
  • Students moving from interstate or a non-government school should ask their new school to apply for a Chromebook — they may need to complete an acceptance form.
  • BYOD is permitted alongside the Chromebook for specialist subjects (IT, Media, Physics, Graphic Design) where a more powerful Windows/Mac device is useful. Chromebook handles standard curriculum well.
  • Canberra High School specifically notes BYOD is not recommended due to Wi-Fi connection issues — the school Chromebook is preferred there.
  • Google Workspace (Google Classroom) is used across all ACT public schools. SchoolsNET — the shared secure Wi-Fi — uses the same login at any ACT public school.
  • ACT senior qualification: BSSS (Board of Senior Secondary Studies) — roughly equivalent to the HSC or VCE.
  • If a family arrives at a Canberra DFV shelter and needs to enrol children: the good news is they will not need to buy a laptop for any ACT government school.
Victoria (VIC)

VIC secondary schools cover Years 7–12 in one school. BYOD is for secondary students only — primary families no longer required to supply devices (2025 reform).

  • Free software (important): Microsoft Office 365 and Adobe Creative Cloud are both free via EduSTAR for all VIC Dept of Education students. Do NOT purchase these separately — some schools explicitly warn against it.
  • Equity requirement: VIC Dept policy requires schools to ensure no student is disadvantaged due to cost. Schools must offer alternatives. Contact the school about loan devices, payment plans, or hardship provisions. Some schools explicitly offer loan devices.
  • Health Care Card (HCC) discounts: Some VIC schools (e.g. Wodonga Senior Secondary College) offer a $200 HCC discount voucher through LWT. Always ask the school if they have HCC provisions.
  • Interest-free payment plans: Many VIC schools using LWT offer 12-month interest-free payment plans — useful for families who cannot afford upfront device purchase.
  • Melbourne High School and Mac Robertson Girls run school-managed 1:1 programs — BYOD is NOT permitted and devices cannot connect to school network.
  • EduNet, LWT and JB Hi-Fi Education are the main VIC purchasing portals. Refurbished devices are explicitly accepted at some schools (e.g. Clyde Secondary College).
  • No charging at some schools: Some VIC schools (e.g. Beechworth SC) do not permit charging at school for OH&S reasons — device must arrive fully charged.
  • VCE (Victorian Certificate of Education) is the senior qualification at end of Year 12.
